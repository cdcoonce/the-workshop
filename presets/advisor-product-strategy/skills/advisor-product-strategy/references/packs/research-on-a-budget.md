# Research on a Budget

**Topic:** Real user signal without a research team — interview technique beyond basics, small-N validity (when N=5 is enough and when it lies), scrappy instrumentation, continuous discovery at startup speed, evidence cheap enough to gather weekly.
**Researched on:** 2026-07-19
**Staleness:** 12 months default. Entries tagged `fast-moving` go stale in 6 months (tooling/vendor landscape shifts).

**Load me when…**
The conversation is about sizing or defending a study N — usability rounds, interview counts, per-segment cells, any "is 5 users enough" fight; standing up or rescuing a weekly discovery cadence at a company with no research headcount; interview technique past Mom Test basics — compliment deflection, commitment scoring, specific-instance timelines; or scrappy evidence infrastructure — call mining, session replay, autocapture, painted doors, low-traffic experimentation. Also load for any "we don't have the traffic/users to test" objection.

---

### 1. Nielsen's 5 is a cadence contract with a narrow jurisdiction — **Contested**

- **Claim:** Treat the 5-user number as an iteration-cadence argument, not a coverage guarantee: prefer three rounds of 5 with fixes between rounds over one round of 15 — the original ROI math is about catch-fix-retest loops. And keep it inside its jurisdiction: qualitative problem-discovery, one homogeneous population, same tasks, same app. The moment a rate leaves your mouth ("4 of 5 completed it"), you're drifting toward quant territory.
- **When it applies:** Formative usability on a scoped flow with comparable users; weekly iteration loops where each round feeds fixes into the next.
- **When it fails / contested:** The iteration framing is solid across camps (NN/g and UIE independently land on ongoing small rounds). The contested part is the absolutist number-trigger: Sauro's small-sample statistics work shows completion rates, adjusted-Wald intervals, and t-tests are legitimate at n=5-10 — small N limits you to big effects and wide intervals, it doesn't forbid numbers. Even NN/g's Moran allows raw descriptive counts from qual sessions so long as no population generalization is implied. 30-40+ is the bar for narrow-interval benchmarking, not for uttering a number.
- **Source:** [Why You Only Need to Test with 5 Users (Nielsen, NN/g)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/why-you-only-need-to-test-with-5-users/); [How Many Test Users? (Nielsen, NN/g)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/how-many-test-users/); [Why 5 Participants Are Okay in a Qual Study, Not a Quant One (Budiu, NN/g)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/5-test-users-qual-quant/); [Eight Is Not Enough (Perfetti, UIE)](https://articles.centercentre.com/eight_is_not_enough/). Counter: [Best Practices for Statistics on Small Sample Sizes (Sauro, MeasuringU)](https://measuringu.com/small-n/); [Collecting Metrics During Qualitative Studies (Moran, NN/g)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/metrics-qualitative/).
- **Date:** Sources 2000-03-18 / 2012-06-03 / 2021-07-25 / 2001-06-18; counters 2013-08-13, 2021-06-13. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 2. Size N by the problem frequency you'd act on — and buy variance insurance where a miss is expensive — **Contested**

- **Claim:** The 85% headline is downstream of p=.31 from 1993-era interfaces — 5 users only catches the loud problems. On a polished production surface (released apps: per-problem frequency probably <10%), size by the minimum problem frequency you'd act on and let the binomial set N. And on revenue-critical flows (checkout, onboarding, billing), prefer 10 users over 5 — not for more findings but to kill variance: Faulkner's random draws of 5 from a 60-user pool caught 55%-99% of problems; 10 floored the worst draw at 80%, 20 at 95%. You don't get the average, you get one draw.
- **When it applies:** Production surfaces past the loud-problem stage; any flow where one unlucky draw ships a costly miss. 5 stays right for cheap weekly iteration on rough surfaces.
- **When it fails / contested:** The Spool & Schroeder 35%-with-5-users anchor is disputed on interpretation: defenders (Sauro's history, NN/g's 83-project review) attribute the low yield substantially to open-ended, self-directed tasks across four sites — violating the same-tasks assumption and inflating the problem universe — not to production surfaces per se. Faulkner's variance numbers are unrefuted; note the "more users add little" defense addresses average yield, not draw-to-draw variance.
- **Source:** [Faulkner 2003, Beyond the five-user assumption](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/BF03195514) (primary, verified via PubMed PMID 14587545); [A Brief History of the Magic Number 5 (Sauro, MeasuringU)](https://measuringu.com/five-history/); [Spool & Schroeder summary (HFI)](https://www.humanfactors.com/newsletters/enough_is_enough.html). Counter: same Sauro history + [NN/g 2012](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/how-many-test-users/).
- **Date:** Sources 2003 / 2010-07-21 / 2001; counters 2010-07-21, 2012-06-03. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 3. Problems hit user subsets, not uniform draws — recruit for diversity, split personas into cells — **Contested**

- **Claim:** The 5-user math assumes every user has equal odds of hitting every problem; Woolrych & Cockton showed that's false — problems cluster in subgroups with particular knowledge, workflows, contexts. So prefer recruiting for user diversity over adding lookalikes when you suspect risk clusters, and for multi-persona SaaS run parallel mini-studies (3-4 per segment, Nielsen's own caveat) over one blended 5-pool that averages personas into nobody.
- **When it applies:** Broad-SaaS with distinct admin/end-user/buyer roles; any study where the risky behavior lives in a subgroup you might not have sampled.
- **When it fails / contested:** 3-4-per-segment cells inherit the same small-N fragility on complex products — the small-sample critics (Faulkner's 55-99% spread, Spool & Schroeder's serious problems still surfacing after dozens of users) mean per-segment cells that small only catch each persona's high-frequency problems. Treat per-segment as triage that beats blending, not a warranty.
- **Source:** Woolrych & Cockton 2001 (IHM-HCI), corroborated verbatim in [Sauro's history (MeasuringU)](https://measuringu.com/five-history/); [Nielsen's homogeneity caveat, NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/why-you-only-need-to-test-with-5-users/). Counter: [MeasuringU five-history](https://measuringu.com/five-history/) + [five-users](https://measuringu.com/five-users/).
- **Date:** Sources 2001 / 2010-07-21 / 2000-03-18. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 4. Detection math ≠ saturation math — defend N by method, not by ritually citing Nielsen — **Contested**

- **Claim:** Don't import 5 into generative discovery. For surfacing themes in one homogeneous segment, budget ~12 interviews for code saturation and expect 16-24 before you understand what the themes mean (Guest et al.; Hennink's code-vs-meaning split; 2022 systematic review lands on 9-17 for homogeneous populations). N=5 discovery is a directional gut-check, not saturation. And when a stakeholder pulls the Nielsen card, answer with method-local norms: Caine's audit of every CHI 2014 paper found the modal sample is 12 and that N legitimately varies by study type and setting.
- **When it applies:** Generative interviews, theme-surfacing, cross-segment metatheme work (that needs 20-40); stakeholder N-fights where you need a citable non-Nielsen answer.
- **When it fails / contested:** Braun & Clarke argue saturation-as-a-number is incoherent for reflexive thematic analysis — meaning is generated through interpretation, not excavated — so these are empirical benchmarks for codebook-style analysis of homogeneous samples with narrow objectives, not universal thresholds. Caine's data is CHI 2014 vintage: use it for the norms-vary-by-method reframe, not as a current benchmark.
- **Source:** [Guest, Bunce & Johnson 2006](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1525822X05279903); Hennink, Kaiser & Marconi 2017; Hennink & Kaiser 2022 review; [Hagaman & Wutich 2017](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1137557); [Caine, Local Standards for Sample Size at CHI (CHI '16)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2858036.2858498). Counter: [Braun & Clarke, To saturate or not to saturate?](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/2159676X.2019.1704846).
- **Date:** Sources 2006 / 2017 / 2022 / 2016; counter 2019. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 5. The weekly habit is an infrastructure problem — recruiting automation is discovery-ops investment #1 — **Contested** — `fast-moving: 6mo`

- **Claim:** Build the standing recruit pipeline (in-product intercept, support-ticket handoff, reusable panel, scheduled outreach) BEFORE committing to a weekly cadence, and make recruiting automation your first discovery-ops spend — ahead of any repository, tagging taxonomy, or synthesis tooling. Recruiting lag is the documented first crack in every failure account; repositories are the classic premature-ops purchase, optimizing the part of the loop that wasn't broken.
- **When it applies:** No-researcher org committing to continuous discovery; any discovery-ops budget conversation.
- **When it fails / contested:** Two honesty problems. The specific "dies around week 8" timeline exists in exactly one source — Great Question, a vendor selling recruiting automation — corroborated only by another recruiting vendor's five-interview study; the direction (logistics, not willpower, binds) is convergent, the week number is a vendor narrative. And "first" is well-supported but "only" overstates: synthesis burden is the documented #2 killer in the same sources, so pair the recruit channel with a lightweight synthesis habit — just don't buy tooling for it first.
- **Source:** [Continuous discovery habits (Great Question)](https://greatquestion.co/blog/continuous-discovery-habits); [How Teams Do Continuous Discovery Research Today (User Interviews)](https://www.userinterviews.com/blog/continuous-discovery-research-report); Torres on automating recruiting ([Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/teresa-torres-on-how-to-interview)). Counter: the same two vendor sources, read critically (synthesis = #2 struggle).
- **Date:** Sources 2026-05-06 / 2023-11-07 / 2022. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 6. Anchor on a segment plus a directional outcome — and "no users yet" is a tactics gap, not a blocker

- **Claim:** Anchor weekly discovery on a customer segment plus a directional outcome, never on the founding idea — weekly touchpoints pointed at a pet idea just industrialize confirmation bias via escalation of commitment; every sunk week deepens the investment in validating the original concept. And pre-product, recruit from watering holes (subreddits, communities, conferences) and cheap paid social ads rather than deferring discovery until launch — which is exactly when the commitment trap has already closed.
- **When it applies:** Pre-product and pre-PMF founder-led discovery; any "we'll do research after we ship" conversation.
- **When it fails / contested:** No published counter to the anti-confirmation-bias mechanism was found (the only critical material targets team power dynamics, out of scope). Boundary condition: B2B/enterprise segments are hard to reach via paid social — that changes the tactic (communities, conferences, warm intros), not the principle. Generic non-probability-sampling caveats apply to ad-recruited participants; screen for professional respondents.
- **Source:** [How Continuous Discovery Works (and Doesn't) in Early-Stage Startups (Torres, Product Talk)](https://www.producttalk.org/discovery-in-startups/) — attributed, verified verbatim for both halves.
- **Date:** Source 2022-04-20. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 7. Strip the ceremony, keep the decision coupling — **Contested**

- **Claim:** At two-person-product-org scale, run a de-ceremonialized Torres loop — same recruit source every week, same interview format, same-day synthesis — instead of the faithful full framework; no surveyed team practices it verbatim, and the survivors are the ones who lightened it. Then score the practice by decisions changed, not touchpoints logged — discovery theater (calendar full, repo tagged, zero decisions anyone can name) launders assumptions as evidence and is worse than no discovery. Solo operator format: 25-minute single-topic sessions with a named decision due after synthesis.
- **When it applies:** Tiny product orgs; solo research operators; any practice review where activity metrics look healthy but nobody can name a decision that moved.
- **When it fails / contested:** The 25-minute format is one practitioner's pattern (Get Product People), not consensus — NN/g's standalone-interview baseline is ~60 minutes with rapport and probing emphasis, which short single-topic sessions constrain. "Decisions per month" as a unit is extrapolation; no source names the period. And the de-ceremonialization finding is descriptive (survivors adapt), not proven causal.
- **Source:** [User Interviews continuous-discovery report](https://www.userinterviews.com/blog/continuous-discovery-research-report); [OST anti-patterns (LogRocket)](https://blog.logrocket.com/product-management/opportunity-solution-trees-a-list-of-anti-patterns-to-avoid/); [Get Product People](https://www.getproductpeople.com/blog/continuous-product-discovery-setup-cadence-tools); [Ainna](https://ainna.ai/resources/faq/product-discovery-faq). Counter: [User Interviews 101 (NN/g)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/user-interviews/).
- **Date:** Sources 2023-11-07 / 2025-10-22. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 8. Pre-PMF dose: founder-led volume, batched — and a weekly drip beats quarterly sprints — **Contested**

- **Claim:** Pre-PMF, prefer founder/PM-led interview volume (10-20+ conversations a week, paid in founder calendar) over hiring or outsourcing — Gothelf's case studies run 2-4/day pre-launch to 30/week scaling, all team-run. Manage the real cost, flow-state fragmentation, by batching into one or two days. Defend the weekly cadence with sprint math: ~5-6 hours a week of discovery against ~240 engineering hours for one misdirected two-week sprint of three engineers — killing one wrong sprint a quarter pays for the year.
- **When it applies:** Pre-PMF with no research headcount; anyone proposing quarterly research sprints as the primary evidence channel (sprints stay useful as deep-dives that complement the drip).
- **When it fails / contested:** Founder-interviewer bias is structural — social desirability (people tell the founder what they want to hear), confirmation bias in what gets remembered, selection bias from network recruiting. Mitigations: third-party recruiting, blinded transcript review, pre-registered kill criteria. Caveat on the critics: the loudest 2026 statements come from vendors selling moderated-research tooling. And Strunk's two-startup comparison is an n=2 coaching anecdote — present it as attributed practitioner economics, not a study.
- **Source:** [How 3 startups built their customer discovery practice (Gothelf)](https://jeffgothelf.com/blog/how-3-startups-built-their-customer-discovery-practice/); [Continuous Discovery (Strunk)](https://www.christianstrunk.com/blog/continuous-discovery); [Ideaplan](https://www.ideaplan.io/compare/discovery-sprint-vs-design-sprint). Counter: [Founder Bias in Customer Research (User Intuition)](https://www.userintuition.ai/reference-guides/founder-bias-customer-research-how-to-fix-it/); [Intairview](https://intairview.com/blog/why-founders-ask-biased-questions).
- **Date:** Sources 2023-09-11 / 2026-02-07; counters 2026-03-31. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 9. In the room: force specific-instance timelines, deflect compliments, hunt disconfirmation — **Contested**

- **Claim:** Prefer a timeline reconstruction of one specific past instance ("walk me through the last time X happened, from the top") over any generalized preference or frequency question — generalities return an idealized self; refuse the general answer and re-anchor. And actively deflect every compliment, then invert toward failure modes ("why wouldn't this work for you?") — a transcript with no negative information indicts the interviewer, not the product. The senior job in the room is making _no_ cheap to say.
- **When it applies:** Every discovery conversation; doubly so in politeness-heavy relationships (existing customers, warm intros, anyone who knows it's your product).
- **When it fails / contested:** The stated mechanism "people recall specifics accurately" is refuted — NN/g's position is that even specific-instance recall is incomplete and reconstructive, and interviews stay attitudinal (reported, not observed, behavior). Soften to: specifics are _less confabulated_ than generalities. When you need actual behavior, pull logs or observe — don't re-litigate memory. Minor form note: "what would stop you from switching?" is mildly hypothetical; strict practice rephrases toward past switching behavior.
- **Source:** Torres story-based interviewing ([Shortform summary](https://www.shortform.com/blog/teresa-torres-customer-interviews/)); Fitzpatrick via [mtlynch's Mom Test report](https://mtlynch.io/book-reports/the-mom-test/); [Looppanel](https://www.looppanel.com/blog/customer-interviews); [Gupta, Advanced Techniques](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/customer-interviews-advanced-techniques). Counter: [User Interviews: How, When, Why (NN/g)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/user-interviews/); [Don't Listen to Users (Nielsen)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/first-rule-of-usability-dont-listen-to-users/).
- **Date:** Sources 2023-09-04 and undated practitioner pages; counters undated / 2001-08-04. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 10. Weigh evidence by what it cost the participant — **Contested**

- **Claim:** Score conversations by what was actually spent — time, reputation, or money — over anything said; a meeting ending without a concrete costly next step is a failed data point, and "sounds great, keep me posted" is a soft no. When words and logs conflict, prefer behavior traces (repeat usage, drop-off, what they used before, referrals, complaints) — and read complaints as engagement: people complain when they care, silence is the churn signal. When politeness pressure is high, put an artifact in front of them (clickable prototype, sketch, the competitor's product) — reactions to a thing are harder to perform than answers to a question — and run the guide as a roadmap, not a script.
- **When it applies:** Solution-stage validation and B2B sales-adjacent conversations; the polite-early-users-who-don't-return problem; any synthesis where verbatims and usage disagree.
- **When it fails / contested:** Three boundaries. (1) Commitment-scoring over-extends into generative research — with no product to advance toward, demanding a costly next step contaminates the data; mtlynch (a source for the claim) names the internal tension with Fitzpatrick's own no-pitching rule. Score _validation_ meetings this way, not every conversation. (2) Complaints-as-engagement rests on a single Substack post plus vocal-minority risk — complaints are still verbal, attitudinal data; treat as directional. (3) The Mom Test "showing your idea invites compliments" warning targets Q&A pitching, not observed interaction with an artifact — compatible, but keep the artifact session observational.
- **Source:** [Mom Test ch.5 summary (dev.to)](https://dev.to/egepakten/the-mom-test-chapter-5-commitment-and-advancement-1k27); [mtlynch](https://mtlynch.io/book-reports/the-mom-test/); [Startup Launch OS](https://startuplaunchos.substack.com/p/my-early-users-give-polite-feedback); [Gupta](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/customer-interviews-advanced-techniques); behavior-over-words anchor: [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/first-rule-of-usability-dont-listen-to-users/). Counter: [mtlynch criticisms section](https://mtlynch.io/book-reports/the-mom-test/); [NN/g user-interviews](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/user-interviews/).
- **Date:** Sources 2026-03-14 / 2023-09-04 / 2001-08-04. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 11. Mine the calls you already record — one source, five fields, 24 hours — **Contested** — `fast-moving: 6mo`

- **Claim:** Prefer mining recorded sales/support calls as a standing weekly research feed over scheduling net-new interviews for every question — raw customer talk-time isn't the scarce resource anymore, extraction is. Access-to-recordings is worthless until someone extracts structured fields: Gong-style tools structure calls for the rep's pipeline, not your discovery question. The survivable discipline: one call source, a fixed five-field extraction (problem, impact, workflow, requests, urgency), processed within 24 hours (~10 min/call) — compounds into queryable evidence in 6-8 weeks. Company-wide conversation-intelligence rollouts stall before yielding a single insight.
- **When it applies:** SaaS with recorded calls and zero research headcount; recurring "what are customers saying about X across accounts" questions; keeping weekly cadence alive when scheduling stalls.
- **When it fails / contested:** Torres (2026) classes sales-call notes and support tickets as "low-value signals" that rarely carry enough strength to tell you what to build — secondhand, sales-framed, and the danger is projecting your own assumptions onto weak signal. Read call mining as a supplement to direct story-based interviews, never the substitute. Also note the vendor-independence is thin: Usersnap sells feedback tooling.
- **Source:** [Customer Call Recordings Into Product Insights (Usersnap)](https://usersnap.com/blog/customer-calls-to-product-insights/); Gong's own rep-centric framing ([Gong blog](https://www.gong.io/blog/sales-call-recording)). Counter: [Quality of Evidence (Torres, Product Talk)](https://www.producttalk.org/quality-of-evidence-all-things-product-podcast-with-teresa-torres-petra-wille/).
- **Date:** Sources 2026-04-13; counter 2026-07-14. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 12. Pre-PMF analytics floor: recordings, three metrics, autocapture hybrid, threshold proxies — **Contested (proxy half)** — `fast-moving: 6mo`

- **Claim:** Pre-PMF, prefer session recordings plus a 3-metric dashboard (signups, pageviews, core-feature usage) over funnel optimization or any warehouse/BI buildout — at hundreds-to-thousands of events, funnel work is premature optimization that hides bigger problems, and the warehouse gets rebuilt after the pivot. Instrument with autocapture plus 5-10 deliberately manual core events, not a hand-built tracking plan — manual-only trades data quality you don't need yet for discovery latency you can't afford (Amplitude shipping autocapture after arguing against it is the market's concession). Define the core-usage metric as a non-averaged threshold proxy ("% of new users who do X ≥ n times in first session") — averages let a small subset move the number without the experience improving.
- **When it applies:** Pre-PMF, shipping weekly, no data headcount; picking the first analytics stack; designing the weekly-review metric.
- **When it fails / contested:** Vendor provenance everywhere — PostHog sells replay, and all three analytics vendors now sell autocapture (the hybrid recommendation cutting against pure marketing is the saving grace); autocapture's real downsides stand (generic event names, silent breakage on UI changes, PII risk). On proxies: the "pair with a counter-metric" insurance is a synthesis, not Biddle — and Spotify's Confidence team prescribes the stronger guard: demote proxies from targets to diagnostics, run several, validate against the north star with long-term holdouts, because a single counter-metric pairing misses non-linear proxy-outcome breakdown. Biddle's underrated half: finding the _right_ proxy can take six months.
- **Source:** [80/20 of early-stage analytics (PostHog)](https://posthog.com/founders/early-stage-analytics); [Definite](https://www.definite.app/blog/data-analysis-for-startups); [Is autocapture still bad? (PostHog)](https://posthog.com/blog/is-autocapture-still-bad); [Amplitude](https://amplitude.com/explore/data/autocapture-vs-manual-tracking); [Mixpanel](https://mixpanel.com/blog/autocapture-analytics/); [Proxy Metrics (Biddle)](https://gibsonbiddle.medium.com/4-proxy-metrics-a82dd30ca810). Counter: [When Proxy Metrics Break (Spotify Confidence)](https://confidence.spotify.com/blog/proxy-metrics).
- **Date:** Sources 2023-02-09 / 2026-03-18 / 2022-07-25 / 2025-02-03 / 2019-07-12; counter 2026-05-12. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 13. Painted doors: kill-switch reading, honest hygiene, shrinking jurisdiction — **Contested (thin-real half)** — `fast-moving: 6mo`

- **Claim:** Read take-rate asymmetrically: a dead door on decent traffic is strong kill evidence; a busy door is weak build evidence — the click says demand exists, nothing about why, retention, or willingness to change workflow (Gilad's evidence ladder scores a smoke test _below_ 20+ interviews or a concierge MVP). Run doors clean: small-percentage rollout (~5-10% of one segment), instant honest "not built yet — want in?" reveal, follow-up email, and a ~6-month cooldown per segment — the hygiene is a validity instrument, since resentment-noise pollutes the take-rate. And in the AI-codegen era, prefer shipping a thin real version for most app-layer features; reserve doors for the genuinely expensive stuff — deep integrations, infra-level features, multi-platform builds, third-party API negotiations.
- **When it applies:** Demand-sizing before a big commitment; big-ticket build/kill decisions; recruiting a beta cohort from clickers.
- **When it fails / contested:** The thin-real-default is one vendor's position (Userpilot). The counter has teeth: IEEE Spectrum practitioners report AI-built code that looks functional but fails in implementation, that specifying intent can cost more than building, and that anything shipped needs expert review — plus a shipped thin feature carries maintenance and support surface, and is a commitment that's harder to remove than a mock. A door measures demand at near-zero carry cost. Also ProdPad's do-not-use list: live UX users depend on, expected/table-stakes features, compliance-heavy products.
- **Source:** [Painted-Door Tests (Stanier)](https://crstanier.medium.com/a-product-managers-guide-to-painted-door-tests-a1a5de33b473); [Evidence scores (Gilad)](https://itamargilad.com/evidence-scores-the-acid-test-of-your-ideas/); [Fake Door Testing in the Vibe-Coding Era (Userpilot)](https://userpilot.com/blog/fake-door-testing/); [ProdPad](https://www.prodpad.com/glossary/fake-door-testing/). Counter: [Vibe Coding (IEEE Spectrum)](https://spectrum.ieee.org/vibe-coding).
- **Date:** Sources 2019-07-23 / 2017-08-12 / 2026-06-25; counter 2025-04-08. Researched 2026-07-19.

### 14. Small-N experimentation: hunt big effects or don't experiment — **Contested**

- **Claim:** Retire "we don't have the traffic to A/B test." Power scales with sqrt(N) but linearly with effect size — 10k users hunting +15% has more power than 100M hunting +0.1% (Chan's math: 10,000x fewer users = 100x less power, against a 150x bigger target effect). The low-traffic playbook: radical variants only (offer, pricing, page concept — never button colors), micro-conversions as the test metric, a hard ~6-week cap then kill; below roughly 5-10 conversions/week, skip controlled experiments entirely and drop to qual. Half the value is catching harm — roughly a third of experiments come back negative.
- **When it applies:** Low-traffic products where the candidate change is a big swing — which is what an early-stage company should be shipping anyway. The honest version of the objection is "we can't test small things," and that's a feature.
- **When it fails / contested:** Gelman & Carlin's winner's curse: when the true effect is smaller than the ≥15% you're hunting (it usually is), the small-N test is de facto underpowered — statistically significant "wins" exaggerate true effects 2-3x at 20-30% power (Type M) and can carry the wrong sign (Type S). Small-N experimentation is sound for catching large harms and confirming genuinely large effects; treat its positive results as inflated by default. Also FastStrat's stricter 350+/variant bar implies 5-10 conversions/week is the floor for _skipping_, not sufficiency for testing.
- **Source:** [You Don't Need Large Sample Sizes (Chan, Statsig)](https://www.statsig.com/blog/you-dont-need-large-sample-sizes-ab-tests); [Low-traffic A/B (VWO)](https://vwo.com/blog/ab-split-testing-low-traffic-sites/); [Optimizely low-traffic tips](https://support.optimizely.com/hc/en-us/articles/4410283325325-Test-tips-for-low-traffic-sites); [FastStrat](https://faststrat.ai/ab-testing-small-business-when-worth-it/). Counter: [Type S and Type M Errors (Gelman & Carlin)](https://sites.stat.columbia.edu/gelman/research/published/retropower_final.pdf).
- **Date:** Sources 2024-12-12 / 2026-03-16 / 2024-02; counter 2014-11. Researched 2026-07-19.
